DSGN 100: April - June 2020

THE CHELLENGE

2020 was a year where most students & job-seekers found themselves in a rut when it came to securing a job. My team and I tried developing a new approach that would make the process of job searching a little bit easier and more systematic for them.

MY ROLE

The journey of creating Bluum was undoubtedly a group effort. From the early user research to the final high-fidelity model, all five team members tried to help with every aspect of the project. However for me, I was specifically assigned to the role of user research analysis. For this role, I was in charge of extracting valuable insights from user interviews and prototype testing, along with the creation of the user personas.

starge 1

discovery

Knowing that the job hunting field is broad and saturated, our team took the first step by formulating , “How could we adapt ourselves to this new setting of learning and working?” We already had ideas in mind on the different problems being experienced in this new work setting, but it is always best to hear from communities outside our team since this will provide a more accurate representation of the job hunting market at the time.

Identifying the emerging problems for student job seekers

The main community we targeted for our data gathering process was the UCSD student community because they best represented our future customers: people looking for employment. After sending out the survey and analyzing the responses, our team concluded that students at the time struggled with finding motivation to apply given the stressful environment of the pandemic, and needed innovative methods for job hunting.

When it comes to the numerics of the surveys, 62% of respondents expressed their frustration about losing focus and not being productive when it comes to finding a job. Whereas the other 38% had a neutral or even positive feeling towards the matter. Here are some direct words from our respondents:

  • “I feel very unmotivated and unproductive. It reminds me of bad memories from high school as I feel disconnected from friends.”
  • “Down, but not entirely sure how to seek for these internships and jobs at home. I feel very uneasy and nervous about whether it'll even be possible to do work in the summer.”

With the insights we have gathered, we used them to determine the initial direction our project will be heading towards through a written problem statement: How do we help connect new grads and build their skillset to the limited opportunities out there in the job industry? Here are the main points we kept in mind moving forward from our project discovery phase:

  • What are some ways people try to deal with their frustration?
  • Are there already pre-existing solutions that solve these frustrations?
  • If we were people looking for employment, what are the features we would want in a job-search platform?

Stage 2

Setting sail

When it came to our competitive analysis, we wanted to deepen our understanding of what already exists and what is still missing in job-hunting platforms. Right away, we realized that even though we started the project during the rise of the pandemic, job-hunting is a niche that is universal and has been around regardless of what is occurring in the world. But that does not mean we cannot develop a unique job-hunting concept that could add to the market.

We decided to analyze five popular job-hunting platforms (LinkedIn, Indeed, Handshake, etc.) and our main takeaway was that the market lacked multifunctional platforms that catered towards student job seekers. This is our opportunity area: try to efficiently and systematically present well rounded skill sets (soft skills such as public speaking & hard skills such as coding) of students directly to recruiters.

Walking in their shoes

With the insights we have gained so far from our interview and competitive analysis, we composed two different personas with the first one being the users we are targeting. For our second stakeholder, we wanted to focus on the recruiters and their experience with the hiring process during the pandemic. With these two personas that show the main pain points, goals, and motivations for both the users and stakeholders, we could understand the problems from both perspectives.

With all things considered so far, my team wanted to finalize the three main points we should focus on and attempt to address in our main product. These three point will act as the stepping stones for our team to begin prototyping and eventually create actual solutions for them.

  • Users feel helplessness at the preparation stage where they’re isolated at home and underqualified for positions, and not knowing where to start
  • Students having all their resources exhausted even when it comes to networking due to cancellation of events
  • Application stage: More tedious and difficult than it needs to be with outdated listings or posts that is bombarded with jargons that overwhelms the applicant

Stage 3

Prototyping

Steering the boat

Before diving headfirst and trying to create concrete solutions, I suggested to my team that we should rapidly prototype through the creation of storyboards. The reason being that they are quick to create and can yield a lot of information on what works and what doesn’t. In order to make this possible though, the team and I presented all of the six storyboards we created to twelve interviewees for their feedback. Based off of the twelve interviews, eleven responded with storyboard #1 being their favorite. This strong direction being displayed by our interviewees comes to show that the storyboard itself holds potential and could be used to jumpstart the product creation process.

Stage 4

Product Creation

Our team has finally settled in a main direction, which is a job-hunting mobile app that will provide users with the opportunity to develop their skills while networking. In order for the users to be able to do this, we created and implemented four main features on the app that each plays a role and intertwine with each other.

Create projects: users can create a project of their own liking or use an "approved template" created by recruiters.

Profile: users could take a look at other users’ profile to see what they’re working and if that work interests them

Collaborate: users could then recruit teammates to join their current project or join an entirely new one

Recruiter reach out: Lastly, recruiters are able to see user's finished projects if they decided to choose from one of the recruiter's template. This allows the recruiters to contact potential candidates directly.

Test whenever possible

Even though we already had a set in stone direction for our project, that does not mean we can no longer perform testing to yield insights and further improve our project. This is exactly what we did when we user tested the lo fidelity version on a group of seven students who resembled our targeted audience.

With the information we gathered from our seven interviewees, we concluded as a team that there were two main design decisions that had to be made:

  • Further encouragement of collaboration among diverse skill sets in a project:

In our original model, we had it set up where friends could add their friends to different roles. But during our user testing, we realized that peers usually work with others who are in the same major, presenting a lack of diversity in skillsets. For our new design, our team encouraged reciprocal interaction between those who have different skill sets, but have found common ground when it comes to interest.

  • Users prioritize a project management feature over a profile display.

Our team also agreed right away with this tweak being made because users spend more time working on the actual projects rather than applying. As a result, the importance of having a project management feature outweighs that of demonstrating the user profile. However, our team still wanted to make sure that a user's profile could be accessed with a small icon on the top right.

Stage 5

Further Sharpening

At this point in our project, we had the skeleton and meat of the project all in place. Now it was time to drape the skin on in a way that could enhance our product even further. As nearly half of our interviewees/ respondents had their jobs & internship cancelled due to the pandemic, people were feeling pretty upset and unmotivated. Taking this into consideration, we created a moodboard that gave off a warm feeling but at the same time still grabs the user’s attention enough to have them feel energized and attentive. We used warm orange/yellow colors to convey calmness, while mixing in purple and pink to stand out and have the users remain energetic.

By combining the two moodboard, we came up with the name called Bluum, in the hope of helping our users’ career bloom with more projects to practice on and later more offers from companies. We then created our color palette with purple as our major color, and then yellow and pink to add more dynamic to the theme. We then finished it off with deciding the typefaces and the buttons of different types and different states.

Stage 6

Final Launch

On the final day of class, my team and I led a fifteen minute presentation that encapsulated our entire journey and what our final product has to offer. We started off by announcing our mission statement for the product, and followed it up with a demonstration of the user flow of the app. As the presentation came to an end, I could not help but to think to myself “How lucky I am to be on a team and work on an actual project like this?” Right then and there I realized, I just created a product that would allow other students to do the exact same thing.
